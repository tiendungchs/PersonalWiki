## Introduction

The default mode network (DMN) refers to a distributed set of brain regions that show greater activity during passive fixation than during traditional cognitive tasks. Its discovery was unexpected, prompting the question: What are these areas doing? This question is typically addressed by linking DMN activity to mental phenomena. Numerous studies implicate the DMN across domains (memory, social cognition, emotion, etc.),,,. Whether the DMN serves a unified role or reflects heterogeneous states across tasks and contexts is unclear,,.

This question is particularly difficult to resolve for two reasons. First, conceptual frameworks or ontologies used to understand DMN function are incommensurate. Some accounts focus on psychological domains (social cognition, emotion, memory, etc.). Others emphasize granular, within-domain distinctions, such as self v. other, fear v. anger, or episodic v. semantic memory. Regardless of scope, these constructs are rooted in domain-specific theoretical frameworks that are difficult to reconcile. For example, overlapping nodes (e.g. the anteromedial prefrontal cortex) have been linked to self-representation, fear, and semantic processing. Broader unifying constructs (e.g. self-projection, internal mentation) have been proposed,,, but remain descriptive rather than mechanistic. An integrative framework for understanding DMN function requires an ontological framework that can extend across psychological constructs.

Second, the definition of the DMN varies across models. While some models treat the DMN as a unified network, others divide it into multiple distinct networks,. Parsing the DMN into appropriate units is required to resolve its functional complexity. But this only solves part of the problem. It does not explain why a given parcel supports a particular psychological function. Moreover, refining the parts (i.e. scientific insight by analysis) requires revisiting how those parts operate together as a whole (synthesis). These issues are interdependent: increasingly fine-grained parcellations complicate synthesis when ontologies lack a shared neurocomputational framework.

An alternative approach begins with the DMN’s anatomy, asking what kinds of processing its structure supports rather than what psychological category it maps onto,. While most research on the DMN focuses on large-scale parcellations using functional neuroimaging data, less attention has been paid to how anatomical properties (e.g. laminar architecture, neuron density, distance to sensory input) constrain the computational roles the DMN can play,,,. Understanding how computational neuroanatomy relates to functional brain mapping, however, requires explanatory accounts at multiple levels. We organize our account of DMN function using a levels-of-analysis framework (a) and draw on the concept of a hierarchical generative model — the idea that the brain as a whole constructs and updates internal models to predict incoming input and guide action,.

From this perspective, the DMN is positioned to support broad (i.e. multilevel) updates to the brain’s generative model. Here, we propose that its neurocomputational organization does not follow a traditional chain-of-command hierarchy (b). Instead, the DMN functions more like an orchestra conductor: it broadcasts or distributes long-range predictions across multiple cortical systems, receives selective input from higher-order association areas, and has neuroanatomical features that support dimensionality reduction (i.e. information integration and compression). These features enable the representation of abstract, multilevel priors,,. We then examine the internal organization of the DMN, characterizing its nodes along three neuroanatomical axes. We begin by summarizing key features of hierarchical generative models of brain function. We then situate the DMN within this framework by examining its role in large-scale brain architecture and internal subdivisions, and we conclude by reinterpreting its functional diversity from this perspective.

## A generative hierarchical model of brain function

In a generative model, the brain continuously generates predictions about incoming sensory inputs and treats deviations as feedback signals used to update the generative model,. Prediction and feedback signals are organized hierarchically,,. Higher-level cortical areas send predictions downward, while lower-level regions send feedback upward,. Most cortico-cortical connections are local and support local features of a generative model. In the visual system, for example, higher visual areas send predictions to lower areas (e.g. V2 to V1), while feedback flows in the opposite direction. Consistent with quantitative tract-tracing studies, the majority of cortico-cortical connections are shorter (<10 mm). Sparse long-distance projections support hierarchical integration across heteromodal, motor, and sensory cortices,.

Regions higher in the cortical hierarchy exhibit neuroanatomical features consistent with dimensionality reduction and abstraction, enabling integration across broader spatiotemporal scales,,. Compared to lower-level areas, they contain fewer neurons, broader projections, and slower transmission speeds, partly due to reduced myelination,,,. When inputs from many lower-level regions converge onto neurons with broader receptive fields and longer integration windows, representations become more integrated and lower in dimensionality than their inputs. In this sense, abstraction functions as dimensionality reduction: high-dimensional inputs are recoded into more compact formats. Within hierarchical generative models, these compressed representations serve as higher-level priors that guide the prediction and reconstruction of sensory input,,,.

## The default mode network in a hierarchical predictive processing architecture

The DMN is considered to occupy higher levels of the hierarchy. DMN nodes are anatomically and functionally more distant from primary sensory regions,, and show greater engagement during abstract psychological processing (e.g. memory, emotion, semantic knowledge),,. However, the specific form of its hierarchical organization remains underspecified. We outline two alternatives: a chain-of-command model, characterized by sequential layer-by-layer communication, and an orchestra conductor model, in which broad multilevel downward projections are paired with more selective upward inputs from intermediate areas (b).

Cortical hierarchical organization is reflected in laminar structure and large-scale connectivity. Cortical areas vary in laminar number and differentiation, forming a gradient that tracks hierarchical position,. The highest level is allocortex, which has 3–4 layers and includes the hippocampus and limbic lobe. Dysgranular cortex has 4–6 poorly differentiated layers and occupies an intermediate position. Most cortex is six-layered isocortex, ranging from moderately differentiated eulaminate areas (I–III; multimodal association areas) to highly differentiated koniocortex (primary sensory), corresponding to progressively lower hierarchical levels.

Paquola et al. characterized the laminar and connectivity profiles of human cortical networks using postmortem histology alongside *in vivo* structural and functional measures. Their results showed that - while many networks are dominated by specific laminar types - the DMN spans a broader range. The visual network, for example, is dominated by koniocortex and eulaminate III, consistent with a lower hierarchical position, while the frontoparietal network is dominated by eulaminate II and I, consistent with an intermediate level. In contrast, the DMN comprises eulaminate III, II, and I, as well as agranular cortex, reflecting both its anatomical distance from sensory input (i.e. absence of koniocortex), and its relatively high hierarchical position, but also a breadth of laminar distribution.

Moreover, its connectivity profile is also unique: although not more densely connected overall, the DMN exhibits a relatively broad cross-network projection profile. It receives proportionally greater input from higher-level association areas (e.g. eulaminate I) than from lower-level sensory areas, while its long-range outputs are distributed diffusely across multiple networks and laminar types. These features support an orchestra conductor model of DMN function (b). Rather than operating through a chain-of-command hierarchy, the DMN is positioned to distribute high-level predictions across multiple networks and laminar types simultaneously while selectively receiving input from higher-order areas. This architecture suggests a role in coordinating global updates to the brain’s generative model.

## Three axes of default mode network organization

The DMN has been conceptualized in different ways. Some models treat it as a unified network, others divide it into distinct networks, or even person-specific networks,. Across these approaches, network definitions largely rely on functional magnetic resonance imaging (fMRI)-based voxel assignments. However, the resulting organization varies with data quantity, analytic assumptions (e.g. fixed vs. dynamic assignments; individual vs. group level), and functional context, including task demands or mood states.

A neuroanatomical lens offers a complementary perspective by imposing structural constraints on DMN models. We propose a three-axis model of DMN organization (): a longitudinal (anterior–posterior) axis, a local hierarchical axis, and a layered communication axis. These dimensions may help clarify the network’s internal structure and functional diversity. We apply this framework to anterior prefrontal and posterior midline DMN complexes ().

Second, a local hierarchical axis: Within each complex, subregions vary in laminar architecture,. A similar gradient appears in the anterior medial complex, from agranular perigenual anterior cingulate cortex to more laminated dorsomedial prefrontal cortex, anteriomedial prefrontal cortex, and ventromedial prefrontal cortex. These laminar gradients suggest that each complex contains an internal hierarchy capable of supporting local generative processing with nearby cortical and subcortical targets. Computationally, this organization allows each complex to generate predictions and integrate inputs at multiple levels, partially independent of the other.

Third, a layered communication axis: Barbas and colleagues describe a ‘laminar type rule’ whereby cortical regions preferentially connect to areas with similar laminar profiles (e.g. granular-to-granular, agranular-to-agranular). Applied to the DMN, this principle supports parallel information exchange across hierarchical levels rather than a single serial pathway (). Such an organization permits flexible coordination between anterior and posterior DMN regions, allowing joint contributions to generative modeling without imposing a strict chain-of-command structure. This architecture aligns with a heterarchical network framework, in which local hierarchies operate within each complex while anterior and posterior regions exchange information laterally across levels to support coordinated inference.

Together, these axes offer suggestions for how the DMN’s anatomy can give rise to multiple, functional configurations. Differences across empirical models may reflect emphasis on one axis over another — whether anterior–posterior gradients, local hierarchy, or laminar-type communication. Speculatively, these anatomically grounded dimensions may also help explain person-specific interdigitated networks (e.g. DMNa and DMNb;, given that laminar organization also varies across individuals. In practice, however, these features operate in concert, enabling the DMN to integrate internal and external information across levels of abstraction and dimensionality.

## Revisiting the functional role of the default mode network in cognition

Returning to the question, what does the DMN do?, our framework offers a multilevel account. At a global level, the DMN — acting as an orchestra conductor — distributes predictions across much of the brain. At a local level, the three-axis model predicts that DMN function emerges from how its regions collectively minimize prediction error in a whole-brain context, rather than from fixed function–axis mappings.

Much of the time, sensory input is well predicted by the immediate past, requiring only modest, local adjustments. In these moments, the DMN may play an autopilot role by maintaining a global generative model with minimal perturbation. However, when the immediate past poorly predicts incoming input, the brain requires a more substantial update to its generative model. In such cases, endogenous sources (prior knowledge) are recruited to reconstruct the generative model to more effectively minimize prediction error.

When do vast updates to the brain’s generative model occur? Scene changes in naturalistic films often involve shifts in time, place, or goals that require global model updating,, as do changes in goals (e.g. thermoregulatory to metabolic), mental models to predict others’ behaviors, or affective states,,. This global level account helps explain why the DMN is implicated across seemingly heterogeneous domains — memory, social cognition, semantic processing, emotion, and arousal, within a shared computational framework.

At the level of internal organization, the three-axis framework suggests that the DMN affords multiple functional configurations. Resting-state fMRI studies show that the DMN occupies distinct functional states both during tasks and at rest. Task-based studies likewise reveal dissociations along the anterior–posterior axis,, and within local complexes,,,. The layered communication axis remains harder to test, as methods for parsing laminar types in ways that inform task fMRI are still developing. Together, these findings suggest that the DMN reconfigures across functional states as situations demand.

Notably, most existing paradigms are not designed to test hypotheses grounded in the brain as a generative model. This framework, which suggests that psychological categories are products of generative brain processes (a), has three implications. First, predictions are intrinsic to mental phenomena, not separable from them,. Second, predictions are trained in naturalistic, multisensory environments; stripped-down laboratory tasks may fail to engage their core dynamics. Third, predictions and prediction errors depend on skeletomotor and visceromotor activity, placing action at the center of generative processing,,. Understanding DMN, function therefore, requires ecologically valid paradigms that preserve generative dynamics.

## Summary and conclusion

Progress on the question “what does the DMN do?” requires a multilevel, anatomy-informed framework. Situating the DMN within a hierarchical generative model, we propose that it distributes high-level predictions across cortical systems while receiving selective input from higher-order association areas — functioning as an orchestra conductor. This generative model is recapitulated at local levels within the DMN’s internal structure, organized along three neuroanatomical axes — longitudinal, local hierarchical, and layered communication. Together, these axes support multiple, flexible functional configurations of the DMN.

This perspective unifies findings across cognitive domains by reframing DMN function in terms of generative model maintenance and updating, both globally (integrating information across networks) and locally (differentiating processing within and between complexes). It predicts that variable findings in the fMRI literature may arise when a task selectively emphasizes one axis or configuration over others, and that systematic structure–function relationships can be revealed by tasks explicitly grounded in generative modeling. Under this view, the DMN’s diverse roles in memory, social cognition, emotion, semantic processing, and more reflect manifestations of a common computational architecture.

A similar gradient appears in the anterior medial complex, from agranular perigenual anterior cingulate cortex to more laminated dorsomedial prefrontal cortex, anteriomedial prefrontal cortex, and ventromedial prefrontal cortex. These laminar gradients suggest that each complex contains an internal hierarchy capable of supporting local generative processing with nearby cortical and subcortical targets. Computationally, this organization allows each complex to generate predictions and integrate inputs at multiple levels, partially independent of the other.

Third, a layered communication axis: Barbas and colleagues describe a ‘laminar type rule’ whereby cortical regions preferentially connect to areas with similar laminar profiles (e.g. granular-to-granular, agranular-to-agranular). Applied to the DMN, this principle supports parallel information exchange across hierarchical levels rather than a single serial pathway (). Such an organization permits flexible coordination between anterior and posterior DMN regions, allowing joint contributions to generative modeling without imposing a strict chain-of-command structure. This architecture aligns with a heterarchical network framework, in which local hierarchies operate within each complex while anterior and posterior regions exchange information laterally across levels to support coordinated inference.

Together, these axes offer suggestions for how the DMN’s anatomy can give rise to multiple, functional configurations. Differences across empirical models may reflect emphasis on one axis over another — whether anterior–posterior gradients, local hierarchy, or laminar-type communication. Speculatively, these anatomically grounded dimensions may also help explain person-specific interdigitated networks (e.g. DMNa and DMNb;, given that laminar organization also varies across individuals. In practice, however, these features operate in concert, enabling the DMN to integrate internal and external information across levels of abstraction and dimensionality.

## Revisiting the functional role of the default mode network in cognition

Returning to the question, what does the DMN do?, our framework offers a multilevel account. At a global level, the DMN — acting as an orchestra conductor — distributes predictions across much of the brain. At a local level, the three-axis model predicts that DMN function emerges from how its regions collectively minimize prediction error in a whole-brain context, rather than from fixed function–axis mappings.

Much of the time, sensory input is well predicted by the immediate past, requiring only modest, local adjustments. In these moments, the DMN may play an autopilot role by maintaining a global generative model with minimal perturbation. However, when the immediate past poorly predicts incoming input, the brain requires a more substantial update to its generative model. In such cases, endogenous sources (prior knowledge) are recruited to reconstruct the generative model to more effectively minimize prediction error.

When do vast updates to the brain’s generative model occur? Scene changes in naturalistic films often involve shifts in time, place, or goals that require global model updating,, as do changes in goals (e.g. thermoregulatory to metabolic), mental models to predict others’ behaviors, or affective states,,. This global level account helps explain why the DMN is implicated across seemingly heterogeneous domains — memory, social cognition, semantic processing, emotion, and arousal, within a shared computational framework.

At the level of internal organization, the three-axis framework suggests that the DMN affords multiple functional configurations. Resting-state fMRI studies show that the DMN occupies distinct functional states both during tasks and at rest. Task-based studies likewise reveal dissociations along the anterior–posterior axis,, and within local complexes,,,. The layered communication axis remains harder to test, as methods for parsing laminar types in ways that inform task fMRI are still developing. Together, these findings suggest that the DMN reconfigures across functional states as situations demand.

Notably, most existing paradigms are not designed to test hypotheses grounded in the brain as a generative model. This framework, which suggests that psychological categories are products of generative brain processes (a), has three implications. First, predictions are intrinsic to mental phenomena, not separable from them,. Second, predictions are trained in naturalistic, multisensory environments; stripped-down laboratory tasks may fail to engage their core dynamics. Third, predictions and prediction errors depend on skeletomotor and visceromotor activity, placing action at the center of generative processing,,. Understanding DMN, function therefore, requires ecologically valid paradigms that preserve generative dynamics.

## Summary and conclusion

Progress on the question “what does the DMN do?” requires a multilevel, anatomy-informed framework. Situating the DMN within a hierarchical generative model, we propose that it distributes high-level predictions across cortical systems while receiving selective input from higher-order association areas — functioning as an orchestra conductor. This generative model is recapitulated at local levels within the DMN’s internal structure, organized along three neuroanatomical axes — longitudinal, local hierarchical, and layered communication. Together, these axes support multiple, flexible functional configurations of the DMN.

This perspective unifies findings across cognitive domains by reframing DMN function in terms of generative model maintenance and updating, both globally (integrating information across networks) and locally (differentiating processing within and between complexes). It predicts that variable findings in the fMRI literature may arise when a task selectively emphasizes one axis or configuration over others, and that systematic structure–function relationships can be revealed by tasks explicitly grounded in generative modeling. Under this view, the DMN’s diverse roles in memory, social cognition, emotion, semantic processing, and more reflect manifestations of a common computational architecture.
