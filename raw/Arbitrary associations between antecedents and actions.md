> Some of the psychological papers at the meeting were impressive…, but there were other kinds of papers, too — P.T. Zellerman’s study on the difference in the length of time it took white rats to learn a maze when the corners were curved rather than angular, or Worfel’s paper on the effect of intelligence level on the reaction time of rhesus monkeys. Papers like these made me angry. Money, time, and energy squandered on the detailed analysis of the trivial.

Daniel Keyes

This quotation comes from Charlie Gordon, who, together with a mouse named Algernon, had genius thrust upon him by an experimental brain lesion. We need not share Charlie’s scepticism about spatial-memory and reaction-time studies; he is, after all, fictional. If, as Charlie implies, simply remembering places and reacting faster are relatively unimposing cognitive functions, what kind of behavior might be more impressive, yet amenable to laboratory study?

One potential answer involves conditional motor learning, the arbitrary association between stimuli and actions. This capability supercedes the simple storage of sensory information, such as remembering a place, and it epitomizes the behavioral flexibility that makes up an important aspect of intelligent behavior. Unfortunately, the phrase ‘conditional motor learning’ often generates misunderstanding. The ‘conditional’ term causes confusion with conditioned reflexes, which, unlike conditional motor learning, depend on unconditioned reflexes. The ‘motor learning’ part evokes images of skill acquisition, with which conditional motor learning has even less in common. An alternative terminology relies on propositional logic, ‘if antecedent, then consequent’. Using this vocabulary, we can say that an antecedent maps arbitrarily onto a consequent. When visual cues serve as antecedents and movements as consequents, the result can be called arbitrary visuomotor mapping.

Movements usually have spatial targets, and so visuomotor and visuospatial mapping will be discussed together, emphasizing their common feature: arbitrary use of nonspatial visual cues to produce a clearly observable action. These features have considerable practical value in the laboratory. However, arbitrary visuomotor mapping is no mere laboratory curiosity; it has fundamental importance in human behavior. Without this ability, drivers could not learn to stop at red traffic signals (or, failing that, at least slow down). More generally, signal-guided or symbolically guided actions would be impossible. Arbitrary visuomotor mapping is, we would submit to Charlie Gordon, a higher brain function worthy of ‘money, time, and energy’.

## Ablations

One central issue concerns which neural structures compute these flexible mappings. Neuropsychological research has identified the basic components of a widely distributed neural network underlying this function. The study of arbitrary visuomotor mapping dates back at least to the early 1970s, but the most influential work was carried out more recently, predominantly in the laboratories of Richard Passingham, Michael Petrides and David Gaffan. The work of Passingham and Petrides showed that ablations of the dorsal premotor cortex, a part of the frontal lobe situated between the prefrontal and primary motor areas (PMd in ), caused serious deficits in learning arbitrary visuomotor mappings. For example, monkeys with PMd lesions could not learn to twist a handle in response to a yellow placard, although they performed normally in both twisting the handle and recognizing the yellow cue. Monkeys performed poorly on mappings that had been learned prior to the lesions, as well as on novel mappings presented only after surgery. Ablations of the dorsolateral prefrontal cortex (PFdl) and disconnections of inferior temporal cortex from prefrontal cortex, by cutting the uncinate fascicle (), also caused impairments in learning novel visuomotor mappings,. Furthermore, thalamic lesions intended to disrupt the flow of information from the basal ganglia to the frontal cortex profoundly disrupted this behavior. This work, reviewed in detail elsewhere,, established that the network underlying arbitrary visuomotor mapping included parts of the frontal cortex and basal ganglia.

Bilateral removal of the hippocampal formation, including the dentate gyrus, subiculum and parahippocampal gyrus, also disrupts the acquisition of arbitrary visuomotor mappings, as do fornix transection and other hippocampal lesions. For convenience, these bilateral aspiration lesions will be termed a ‘hippocampectomy’. The use of quotation marks emphasizes the fact that the ablations involved nearby structures, as well as the hippocampus. In these experiments, rhesus monkeys viewed a video screen and grasped a joystick, which was constrained to move in only three directions (). At the beginning of each trial, a visual cue appeared on the screen. After the cue appeared, the monkeys could move the joystick. If that movement was made in the correct direction, reinforcement was delivered, if not, there was no reinforcement. The monkeys’ task was to learn to map three unfamiliar visual cues onto the three possible responses (one cue for each response). The rate at which experienced monkeys could learn 24 new visuomotor mappings each day was impressive. shows learning curves for four rhesus monkeys. On average, they showed substantial learning after only 10 trials (∼3 trials/cue). By the time the monkeys had performed 25 trials (∼8 trials/cue), their error rate had dropped to less than 10%. Some monkeys performed nearly perfectly thereafter. Bilateral ‘hippocampal’ lesions caused a marked disability in learning novel visuomotor mappings in monkeys (), and similar findings have been obtained in humans. In contrast to their poor performance for new visuomotor mappings, the same monkeys performed normally for mappings learned prior to the lesion (). As well as showing that the monkeys can remember familiar mappings, this finding demonstrates that the monkeys can discriminate cues and perform the movements, even after the lesion. Consequently, their difficulty in learning arbitrary mappings cannot be due to fundamental perceptual or motor deficits.

It might be of some interest that the ‘hippocampal’ lesions described above were made to create a comparison group, rather than as the focus of an experiment. (The principal experiment involved ablations of the amygdala to test the now-abandoned hypothesis that it subserves crossmodal associations.) Surprisingly, the principal group yielded no deficit, whereas the ‘comparison’ group proved more interesting. Indeed, these procedures generated an experimentally induced approximation of the amnesia caused by similar surgery in the famous patient, H.M. In the early 1950s, surgery to remove much of his hippocampus and some adjacent structures caused a dramatic problem with his learning and memory. His well-known amnesia is characterized by the preservation of strategies and the ability to learn new skills,, relative preservation of knowledge gained before his surgery and a profound disability in acquiring new knowledge. Experimental psychologists struggled mightily, for two or three decades, to reproduce H.M.’s amnesia in monkeys. For the most part, they failed. The experiments summarized above, together with those demonstrating the ability of monkeys with similar lesions to learn new perceptual and motor skills,, reproduced the main features of his amnesia unintentionally, which is, of course, what happened to H.M.

Thus, it is clear that the ‘hippocampal’ system participates in an arbitrary visuomotor mapping network, together with the frontal lobe and basal ganglia. In addition, other structures might contribute to this behavior. For example, a role in arbitrary mapping for the cerebellum has been reported in humans,. However, because this conclusion has been disputed for monkeys, the cerebellum will not be considered further. The extent to which different components of the network support arbitrary visuomotor mapping as opposed to the arbitrary mapping of visual cues onto places (visuospatial mapping) or onto other visual cues (visuovisual mapping) has yet to be explored systematically and remains an important issue to be addressed. For example, the premotor cortex appears to be necessary for visuomotor but not visuovisual mappings. Likewise, the ‘hippocampus’ might subserve visuospatial but not visuovisual mappings. But other parts of the network clearly have a more-general role in arbitrary mapping, including the processing of nonmotor ‘consequents’, as well as motor ones. For example, lesions involving prefrontal cortex have an effect on both visuovisual and visuomotor mapping,.

## Activated areas

Neuroimaging studies in humans point to the same structures. The premotor cortex and ventral prefrontal cortex show increased activity during behaviors relying on arbitrary visuomotor or visuospatial mapping. Furthermore, as humans learn arbitrary visuomotor mappings, the ventral prefrontal cortex shows learning-related changes in activity, together with parts of the ‘hippocampus’ (the parahippocampal gyrus) and basal ganglia (caudate nucleus), as well as extrastriate visual and cingulate areas.

Like the neuropsychological results discussed above, neuroimaging work suggests that various components of this network contribute differently to this behavior. For example, Passingham *et al.* have argued that, among these structures, the prefrontal cortex has a unique role because only it has information about cues (from visual areas), actions (from premotor areas) and outcome. Some parts of the network might have a very general role in arbitrary mapping, whereas others might be specific for certain kinds of antecedents or consequents. Some brain-imaging studies point to the hippocampus having a more-general role, one involving nonmotor and nonspatial consequents, as well as spatial roles. For example, Henke *et al.* found increased activation in the hippocampus and parahippocampal gyrus during nonmotor associative learning. Petersson *et al.*, who compared less-practiced to well-practiced memories, obtained similar results; and Dolan and Fletcher reported that activation in hippocampal areas reflected the learning of novel word pairs. On this issue, the neuroimaging data diverge from the neuropsychological results, which emphasize spatial factors. However, Dolan and Fletcher reported similar findings for prefrontal cortex, which suggests that its computations, too, extend beyond arbitrary visuomotor mappings to those involving nonmotor consequents. This result accords with those from neuropsychology.

## Actual activity

Neurophysiological studies in monkeys point to the same neural network identified by neuropsychological and neuroimaging studies. For example, Earl Miller and his colleagues contrasted the sensitivity of prefrontal cortex neurons to cues, response direction and the mapping between the two. While recording the activity of each neuron, they first taught the monkey one mapping, then later reversed it (). For activity during the delay period (see ), ∼30% of prefrontal neurons showed selectivity for the cue and ∼20% for the direction of action (for example, eye movements to the left). However, many more prefrontal neurons (∼50%) reflected the specific mapping. The activity of these cells identified the specific combination of cue and movement, such as responses to the left when guided by some cues but not others.

Experimental designs much like those described for the ablation studies () have enabled an examination of neuronal activity as monkeys learn arbitrary visuomotor mappings (). For example, illustrates the activity of a single neuron in a premotor area of the cortex. Its response to the presentation of a visual cue increased systematically as the monkey learned. Neurons such as this comprised more than half of the sampled neurons in premotor cortex. Like most premotor neurons, the activity of this cell was selective for cues that mapped to movements (or targets) in a ‘preferred direction’, in this case, down and to the right.

That original finding was promising, but to interpret the changes in cell activity more rigorously it was necessary to show that the cue always appeared at the same retinocentric coordinates during learning and that the parameters of movement did not change as the monkeys learned. Accordingly, monkeys were required to maintain central fixation prior to cue presentation and throughout a delay period between the stimulus presentation and the response (). Thus, the location of the cue in all relevant spatial coordinate frames could be determined accurately. In this task, the monkeys were required to make an eye movement to one of four potential targets (), and recordings were made in the frontal eye field (FEF) and the supplementary eye field (SEF). The highly stereotyped nature of saccades ruled out the possibility that the movement changed materially during learning. The analysis was limited to correctly executed trials. Because the cue and the movement were the same from the first correctly executed trial to the last, this analysis eliminated the possibility that the learning-related activity changes simply reflected some aspect of the cue or the movement. Generalized behavioral variables such as reward rate, reinforcement expectancy, arousal and motivation can be ruled out on other grounds, at least for the neurons that show a directional preference. For example, the neuron illustrated in increased its activity during learning, but for only two of the four response directions that the monkey learned. Because response direction varied from trial-to-trial, the generalized behavioral variables mentioned above would have changed for all response directions equally. Consequently, those factors alone cannot account for the learning-related evolution in cortical activity.

Incorporating these controls led to results for SEF that were broadly similar to those obtained earlier for premotor cortex. Evolution in activity came in two main forms: learning-dependent, which generally increased during learning () and learning-selective, which usually decreased during learning (), often to the point that the cells became completely inactive. These two types of activity were thoroughly intermingled in the SEF. For the neuronal population illustrated in, a signal could be extracted in the form of a population vector. This single vector represents the net directional signal of a population of cells and is based on the assumption that each cell contributes to or ‘votes’ for movements in its preferred direction. As learning progresses, the SEF population vector becomes more accurate in predicting the direction of the response ().

The learning data reviewed above pertain to premotor cortex, but what of the other brain structures thought to be involved in arbitrary visuomotor mapping? Learning-related evolution of single-cell activity has been reported for each of the major areas: hippocampus and neurons in its vicinity, prefrontal cortex and basal ganglia. Although these pioneering studies differ sufficiently in design to preclude a detailed comparison with premotor areas, a provisional effort seems warranted.

In the hippocampus, Edmund Rolls and his colleagues have reported weak but significant changes in activity levels during the learning of arbitrary visuomotor mappings. The relative weakness of the learning-related activity changes (only a few discharges/second), compared with those seen in premotor cortex (), might reflect differences in experimental design. However, it is tempting to speculate that such small changes are a feature of a sparsely coded network like the hippocampus, whereas the robust activity changes seen in the premotor areas are representative of a densely coded network. Also, learning-selective activity () appears to be more prominent in hippocampus than learning-dependent activity (), whereas the opposite holds for premotor areas, in accordance with the more time-limited role in memory for the hippocampus. In prefrontal cortex, Miller and his colleagues have reported a learning-related time-shift in directional selectivity (). Similar phenomena occur in premotor areas, but they seem to be more common in prefrontal cortex. As for the basal ganglia, Wolfram Schultz and his colleagues reported differences in activity for novel versus familiar mappings in about 30% of striatal neurons. They concluded that neurons in frontal areas show a higher prevalence of learning-related activity change than those in the striatum. They also noted a much higher incidence of learning-related changes immediately after the cue compared with those observed after other task events, which differs from the premotor areas. This finding suggests a special role for the striatum in the earliest phases of a structured event sequence, a finding similar to that obtained for arbitrary auditory-motor mappings in rats. Thus, learning-related evolution in activity has been observed for each major component of the network identified by ablation and neuroimaging studies.

## Concluding remarks

Of what benefit is an arbitrary visuomotor mapping capacity? There are, after all, other forms of visuomotor mapping. In standard visuomotor mapping, for example, a visual cue serves as the target of action. Clearly, arbitrary mapping is not like that, and so it has been termed nonstandard mapping. But there are other forms of nonstandard mapping, too. In one, sometimes termed transformational mapping, the visual cue is not the target of action, but nevertheless guides movement, as if the location of the cue provides an input to an algorithm. In tennis, directing the ball to an opponent’s backhand relies on this type of mapping. Arbitrary mapping represents a more-subtle capacity, one in which the location of the guiding stimulus neither constrains nor determines action. This capability represents an important advance in flexibility over standard and transformational mapping, dependent as they are on spatial information. By liberating the sensory guidance of action from the shackles of spatial information, arbitrary visuomotor mapping allows the selection of any action already within a behavioral repertoire in response to any input. As Passingham has emphasized, this adaptation permits the choice of action based on the prevailing context.

Some indirect consequences of arbitrary mapping might also have been important. Both Lieberman and Bickerton have emphasized the prospect that the evolution of language involved co-opting existing capabilities,. One notable feature of language is the arbitrary nature of words to the information they represent. In arbitrary visuomotor mapping, the relationship between visual cues and output is every bit as arbitrary as the relationship of typical writing and sounds to the information they represent. For example, the word ‘pig’ is not porcine. We do not mean to imply that word meaning evolved from the mapping of sensory antecedents onto motor consequents, although this process could have a role in speech production. Rather, if arbitrary visuomotor mapping represents a special case of a more-general arbitrary mapping ability, then it is likely that this function existed in robust form at the time language evolved. Accordingly, arbitrary mapping might have served as an important preadaptation to language.
