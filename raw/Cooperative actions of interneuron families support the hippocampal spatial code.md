## Abstract

### INTRODUCTION:

The dynamic interactions between the hippocampus and its partner structures create a cognitive map that enables flexible spatial and mental navigation. Single pyramidal cells contribute to this map with their place fields. Most physiological studies assume that the recurrent excitatory system of the CA3 region or the entorhinal grid system generates place fields. In turn, flexibility and remapping are induced by plastic changes in excitatory synapses. An alternative consideration of this flexibility is the contribution of diverse inhibitory microcircuits that shape the place field properties of pyramidal neurons. The multiple interactions among the large diversity of genetically defined interneuron families and their joint effect on the pyramidal neuron can, in principle, regulate the various place field features. Testing this hypothesis requires the simultaneous recording of members from various interneuron families in behaving animals.

### RATIONALE:

Current optogenetic and pharmacogenetic methods typically enable the identification and manipulation of only one or, occasionally, two families in a given experiment. Our first goal was to characterize and validate the interneuron family divisions by demonstrating reliable physiological boundaries among them, using optogenetic identification of the four major interneuron families in individual mice. In turn, these physiological fingerprints allowed us to recognize members of these families in large-scale, unlabeled populations. Thus, we could examine the nature of interactions among them and their relationship to the firing of pyramidal cells in the same experiment. Optogenetic perturbation of the interneuron families, in turn, provided further support for their role in circuit control and their ability to shape the place field features of their target principal cells.

### RESULTS:

We combined multiple-shank silicon probe recordings with optogenetic identification of neurons expressing channelrhodopsin-2 (ChR2) in the four major interneuron families (*Pvalb*, *Sst*, *Vip*, and *Id2* neurons) and pyramidal cells expressing calcium- and calmodulin-dependent protein kinase II alpha (*CaMK2* α) in the hippocampus and neocortex. The physiological features of opto-tagged neurons collected during spontaneous behaviors in the home cage were used to train a machine learning classifier. The high accuracy of physiology-based interneuron classification (>89%) enabled the identification of members of interneuron families in large-scale recording experiments and the study of their interactions. The fractions of the predicted families in unlabeled populations showed a strong correlation with gene expression–based fractions. The identified differentiating physiological features are communicable across different experiments and laboratories. Spiking activity of the members of the four interneuron families showed distinctive correlations with place field features, such as stability, selectivity, generalization, and mutual information between firing rates and the animal’s position. Our family-specific perturbation experiments validated these correlational results. Optogenetic activation of *Pvalb* neurons predominantly suppressed the first half of the place fields of pyramidal cells, whereas *Sst* and *Id2* activation suppressed place field spiking in the second half, demonstrating time-division control of pyramidal cells.

### CONCLUSION:

We demonstrate the reliability of physiological fingerprinting with genetically defined interneuron families. Our findings reveal a fundamental role for the cooperative function of interneuron families in the emergence of the hippocampal cognitive map. Because our physiological fingerprinting strategy is paradigm independent, it is also generalizable to nonspatial functions of the hippocampus and other brain regions.

**Interneuron diversity shapes place field features of the hippocampal cognitive map.** Gene expression clusters neocortical and hippocampal neurons into five families: *Pvalb*, *Sst*, *Id2*, *Vip*, and *CaMK2* α. ---

Identifying the computational roles of different neuron families is crucial for understanding neural networks. Most neural diversity is embodied in various types of γ-aminobutyric acid–mediated (GABAergic) interneurons, grouped into four major families. We collected datasets of opto-tagged neurons from all four families, along with excitatory neurons, from both the neocortex and hippocampus. The physiological features of these neurons were used to train a machine learning classifier, which subsequently inferred specific interneuron families in large-scale recordings. This combined approach enabled the reconstruction of synaptic connectivity motifs across interneuron family members. We further showed that these motifs differentially control the place field features of pyramidal neurons. Our findings attribute a prominent role to interneurons in the formation of a flexible cognitive map.

---

A prominent proposal is that the hippocampus and its partner structures represent a spatial map of the environment ([^1]). The internalization of spatial relationships creates an abstract or cognitive map suitable for learning concepts ([^2], [^3]). This dynamic map enables rapid reorganization (“remapping”) across behavioral contexts, yielding a multitude of cognitive states ([^4]) to serve flexible spatial and mental navigation ([^5]). Most physiological studies have assumed that the map is induced in the recurrent excitatory system of the CA3 region ([^6]) or the entorhinal grid system ([^7]). In that framework, synaptic plasticity within and across regions explains the flexibility and remapping. By contrast, recent thinking attributes importance to the immediate gating and regulatory power of the diverse inhibitory microcircuits in shaping the attractor properties of local circuits, thereby forming flexible cell assemblies ([^8] - [^12]). Hippocampal interneurons exhibit place field–like properties, including on and off fields, theta phase precession, remapping, choice prediction, and learning-related plasticity ([^13] - [^14]), which indicates their active role in shaping these properties ([^9], [^15], [^16]). The large diversity of genetically defined interneuron types ([^17]) also implies a crucial role in their joint impact on pyramidal neurons ([^18] - [^21]). However, the current gap between the molecularly identified families and the physiological features of the recorded or imaged neurons precludes the study of the combined role of interneuron cooperation. Although optogenetic and pharmacogenetic methods enable the identification and manipulation of specific neuronal families in extracellular recordings, typically one or occasionally two families can be identified in a given experiment.

Current interneuron classification schemes ([^11], [^12]) are based on genetic profiling. The numerous “types” ([^17], [^19], [^22] - [^25]) are grouped into four families: *Pvalb*, *Sst*, *Vip*, and *Id2* neurons ([^24], [^25], [^26]), divided further into subfamilies ([^19], [^26] - [^31]). Because these four families account for more than 95% of γ-aminobutyric acid–mediated (GABAergic) neurons in the neocortex and hippocampus ([^24], [^25], [^26]), we compared these families in our experiments.

## Results

### Physiological fingerprinting of interneurons

Using combined light fiber–multiple-shank silicon probes or micro–light-emitting diode (μLED) recording, we optogenetically identified neurons in the neocortex (posterior parietal cortex and primary visual cortex) and the hippocampus of freely moving mice expressing channelrhodopsin-2 (ChR2) selectively in parvalbumin (*Pvalb*), somatostatin (*Sst*), inhibitor of DNA binding 2 (*Id2*), or vasoactive intestinal peptide (*Vip*) GABAergic interneurons or calcium- and calmodulin-dependent protein kinase II alpha (*CaMK2* α) pyramidal neurons ([Fig. 1](#F1), [A](#F1) and [B](#F1); *n* = 7532 units from five neuronal classes; 164 sessions averaging 6.7 ± 1.9 hours per session from 30 mice). These five genetically targeted populations encompass nearly all cortical neuronal diversity ([fig. S1](#SD2), [A](#SD2) and [B](#SD2)) ([^17], [^25]).

Neurons were classified as optically tagged (“opto-tagged”) or untagged on the basis of four statistical criteria (significant rate change, average rate change above normalized threshold, bootstrap, and first-spike jitter; see [Materials and methods](#S12)) ([^9]) ([Fig. 1](#F1), [C](#F1) to [E](#F1), and [fig. S1](#SD2), [C](#SD2) to [E](#SD2)). We trained a decision tree classifier, initially using 17 physiological features from the entire dataset of opto-tagged cortical and hippocampal neurons (*n* = 785). Many of the initially considered features did not contribute to the classification performance ([fig. S1F](#SD2)). Instead, six parameters were of importance—firing irregularity (CV2), autocorrelogram (ACG) rise time, spike width, anatomical location, waveform asymmetry, and firing rate ([Fig. 1F](#F1) and [fig. S1](#SD2), [G](#SD2) and [H](#SD2)). The decision tree trained on these features was highly accurate for the five neuronal families (>89.7%; [fig. S1](#SD2), [I](#SD2) and [J](#SD2)). The low-dimensional projection of these six features ([Fig. 1G](#F1) and [fig. S1K](#SD2)) was similar to the distributions of gene expression–based *t* -distributed stochastic neighbor embedding (*t* -SNE) clusters ([Fig. 1A](#F1)), suggesting a reliable relationship between physiological features of neuronal families and the transcriptome ([^21], [^23]). Decision trees trained on four or five features showed only slightly lower performance ([fig. S2](#SD2), [A](#SD2) and [B](#SD2)).

### Supervised classification of CA1 interneurons

The remaining analysis was confined to CA1 neurons ([fig. S1](#SD2), [G](#SD2) and [H](#SD2)). Hippocampal local field potentials (LFPs) are dominated by theta (5 to 12 Hz), slow-gamma (20 to 50 Hz), mid-gamma (60 to 100 Hz), and fast-gamma (100 to 140 Hz) oscillations and sharp-wave ripples (SPW-Rs), each of which is controlled by various families of interneurons ([fig. S3A](#SD2)) ([^32] - [^33]). On average, all neuron families increased their firing rates during SPW-R \[[Fig. 2A](#F2) and [fig. S3B](#SD2); *P* < 10 <sup>−12</sup>, Kruskal-Wallis (KW) test\], but a fraction of *Id2* and *CaMK2* α neurons were suppressed (25% and 15%, respectively), and the family members were differentially affected by latent features of SPW-Rs ([fig. S3C](#SD2)). The different families also exhibited distinct phase preferences and modulation magnitudes with theta and gamma hippocampal oscillations ([Fig. 2](#F2), [B](#F2) to [E](#F2), and [fig. S3](#SD2), [D](#SD2) to [H](#SD2)).

We added these network features to the decision tree (using 24 features) and trained it with opto-tagged CA1 neurons only ([fig. S4A](#SD2)). A subset of five features—firing irregularity (CV2), ACG rise time, spike width, theta modulation, and spike asymmetry—achieved comparable performance to that of the classifier that included both cortical and hippocampal neurons ([Fig. 2C](#F2) and [fig. S4](#SD2), [B](#SD2) and [C](#SD2)). Next, we applied the classifier trained on opto-tagged neurons to all hippocampal neurons and derived five predicted neuron families ([Fig. 2D](#F2), left). The fractions of these predicted families showed a strong correlation with the gene expression–based fractions, regardless of whether pyramidal cells were included or not ([Fig. 2D](#F2), right).

As a further cross-validation approach, we compared the phase preferences of all opto-tagged and predicted neuronal families with those of all hippocampal oscillation patterns. Although phase preferences were not used for training the decision tree classifier, we observed strong correlations between opto-tagged and predicted neuronal families ([Fig. 2](#F2), [E](#F2) and [F](#F2)).

The five neuronal families considered in our experiments have been characterized in separate previous studies ([^17], [^24], [^34], [^35]), and distinct subgroups within these families have been described ([fig. S5A](#SD2)) ([^17], [^21], [^23]). To demonstrate the general validity of our classifier, we found that a subcluster of *Id2* neurons ([Fig. 2G](#F2)) showed features similar to *Sncg* -expressing *Id2* neurons ([^36]), including suppression of or limited SPW-Rs entrainment and relatively low firing rates ([Fig. 2G](#F2) and [fig. S5](#SD2), [B](#SD2) and [C](#SD2)). This is consistent with the *Id2* expression in both neurogliaform neurons and *Sncg* -expressing cholecystokinin (CCK) basket cells ([^27], [^30], [^37]). Next, we trained a support vector machine (SVM) classifier to differentiate *Id2* neurons into putative *Sncg* and non- *Sncg* classes (100% accuracy; [Fig. 2H](#F2) and [fig. S5](#SD2), [C](#SD2) and [D](#SD2)). By combining our decision tree classifier with the SVM results, we identified putative *Id2-Sncg* subfamily neurons ([Fig. 2](#F2), [G](#F2) and [H](#F2)). Our *Id2-Sncg* neurons also showed similar features to CCK basket cells, including low firing during SPW-Rs, theta peak phase preference, and relatively low gamma entrainment ([fig. S5E](#SD2)) ([^11], [^29]). Finally, we examined the relationship between superficial and deep sublayer CA1 pyramidal neurons, which exhibit distinct gene expression and function ([^37] - [^38]). We defined the middle of the CA1 pyramidal layer as the recording site where SPW-Rs had the largest amplitude ([^38], [^39]) and classified the opto-tagged and predicted *CaMK2* α neurons as superficial (*CaMK2* α-sup) and deep (*CaMK2* α-deep) ([fig. S5F](#SD2)). As expected, superficial *CaMK2* α neurons showed stronger recruitment during SPW-Rs ([^37], [^38]).

### Synaptic and functional coupling of CA1 interneurons and pyramidal cells

Having validated the classifier-derived neuron families, we examined how optogenetic activation of the various families affected the spiking activity of other families ([Fig. 3A](#F3); group statistics in [fig. S6A](#SD2)). The response matrix and the corresponding directed graph in [Fig. 3](#F3), [B](#F3) and [C](#F3), summarize the opto-evoked synaptic results. Activation of *Pvalb*, *Sst*, and *Id2* neurons suppressed all neuron groups in a family-specific manner. *Pvalb* activation most strongly suppressed *CaMK2* α and other *Pvalb* neurons, but *CaMK2* α-deep were more inhibited than *CaMK2* α-sup ([^16], [^37]). *Sst* activation suppressed all neuronal families except the *Id2* –non-Sncg. *Id2* activation suppressed all neuronal families. Activation of *Vip* suppressed *Sst* and disinhibited *CaMK2* α neuron families. *CaMK2* α neuronal activation recruited all neural families to varying extents. Next, we evaluated the relationship between the optogenetic stimulation–based responses and measures of putative functional connectivity. First, we used the mutual information (MI) method, which quantifies the amount of information shared between pairs of signals ([^40]). The adjacency matrix and the resulting graph closely resembled the evoked synaptic results ([Fig. 3D](#F3) and [fig. S6B](#SD2)), and the synaptic and MI measures were correlated (*P* < 10 <sup>−8</sup>, Spearman correlation; [Fig. 3E](#F3) and [fig. S6C](#SD2) for the same analysis varying the temporal bin size). Second, similar results were found when functional connectivity was assessed by fitting generalized linear models (GLMs) to neuronal spike trains ([fig. S6D](#SD2)). Finally, a similar functional coupling interaction matrix was obtained in the neocortex, supporting that circuit motifs are conserved across regions ([fig. S6](#SD2), [E](#SD2) and [F](#SD2)).

### Specialization and cooperativity of CA1 interneuron families during behavior

Armed with the knowledge of a reliable classification of neurons, we examined their position-related spiking activity in a delayed maze-arm alternation task ([Fig. 4A](#F4); 101 sessions in 17 mice). In addition to the classical place fields of pyramidal (*CaMK2* α) neurons, interneurons also showed position-dependent rate modulation ([Fig. 4A](#F4)) ([^8], [^9], [^41], [^42]). Spiking patterns from all interneuron families demonstrated spatially related dynamics, tiling the full length of the maze ([Fig. 4B](#F4) and [fig. S7](#SD2), [A](#SD2) to [C](#SD2)), and the tuning curves remained stable across trials ([fig. S7D](#SD2)). The position coding of place cell assemblies is often tested by their ability to predict the animal’s location in the maze ([^43]). To explore spatial coding by interneurons, we trained a “Consistent Embedding of high-dimensional Recordings using the Auxiliary variables” (CEBRA) model ([^44]) on the animal’s maze position from large-scale hippocampal recordings (five sessions, two mice). Pyramidal neuron populations decoded the animal’s position with high accuracy ([Fig. 4](#F4), [D](#F4) and [E](#F4); the model was trained on 80% of the dataset and tested on the remaining 20%). Because considerably fewer interneurons compared with pyramidal cells were recorded in each session, we down-sampled the pyramidal cells to match the number of interneurons. The decoding effectiveness of the interneuron-only model (combining the predicted *Pvalb*, *Sst*, *Id2*, and *Vip* neurons) was comparable to that of the pyramidal cells when neuron numbers were matched between groups ([Fig. 4E](#F4), left) \[cell family effects: *F* <sub>2, 29</sub> = 4.3, *P* = 0.025; control versus shuffled: *F* <sub>1, 29</sub> = 46.3, *P* < 10 <sup>−7</sup>; interaction: *F* <sub>1, 29</sub> = 4.2, *P* = 0.03, two-way analysis of variance (ANOVA); down-sampled *CaMK2* α versus interneurons only, *P* = 0.63, Fisher’s least significant difference test\]. By conducting a similar analysis while systematically excluding one interneuron family at a time, we observed an increase in the decoding error, with the more-pronounced effect occurring when *Vip*, *Id2*, and *Pvalb* interneurons were excluded ([Fig. 4E](#F4), right) (*P* < 0.03, KW test). The CEBRA model trained on either *CaMK2* α-deep or *CaMK2* α-sup performed similarly ([fig. S7E](#SD2)).

### Interneurons shape spatial features of pyramidal cells

To assess the different contributions of the four interneuron families to position coding, we quantified standard descriptions of spatial features, including the space-rate MI, spatial selectivity (the peak-to-mean ratio of the spatial rate), spatial stability (the correlation between the first and second halves of the session), and context generalization (the correlation between left and right trials), for opto-tagged and predicted family neurons ([Fig. 5A](#F5)) ([^8], [^9], [^42], [^45]). Space-rate MI was comparable between pyramidal cells and interneurons. Of the interneuron families, *Vip* neurons had the highest space-rate MI and spatial selectivity. Spatial-rate MI was also higher in *CaMK2* α-deep than in *CaMK2* α-sup and higher in *Id2* –non- *Sncg* than in *Id2-Sncg*. Spatial stability of *Pvalb* and *Id2* neurons (both non- *Sncg* and *Scng*) was comparable to that of *CaMK2* α place cells. *Sst*, *Pvalb*, and *Id2* –non- *Sncg* rate maps consistently generalized between left and right trials. Notably, the various properties between opto-tagged and predicted neuronal families were correlated ([fig. S8A](#SD2)).

The optogenetic stimulation did not induce overt behavioral changes, as measured by running speed and spatial memory performance ([Fig. 5D](#F5)), ensuring that the induced circuit effects are not confounded by behavioral responses. ## Discussion

We identified the distinct electrophysiological properties of optogenetically identified pyramidal cells and members of the four major interneuron families ([Fig. 1A](#F1)) ([^21], [^24], [^25], [^26]), including their firing rate statistics in different brain states and their contributions to various population activities. The derived physiological fingerprints were then used to classify the specific interneuron families in large-scale recording experiments. The interneuron families and their interactions contributed distinctly to the hippocampal spatial and other properties of hippocampal pyramidal cells.

The four interneuronal populations that we tested— *Pvalb*, *Sst*, *Id2*, and *Vip* —cover all of the major interneuronal families in the isocortex, hippocampal formation, olfactory areas, and cortical subplate ([^17], [^21], [^25], [^35]). However, many subgroups exist within each family ([^12], [^17], [^21], [^23], [^27], [^47]). Our first research goal was to validate the interneuron family divisions by demonstrating reliable physiological boundaries among them. The major biophysical and local circuit properties, spike dynamics, and brain state correlations of these main families were similar across the hippocampus and neocortex but were distinct across families. Our classification algorithm was verified by the optogenetically identified subsets in individual animals, enabling the prediction of all types in large, unlabeled populations. The models achieved high accuracy, with 96% in 10-fold cross-validation and better than 89% for each comparison between opto-tagged and predicted neuronal types. Notably, the physiological features that were used in our decision tree algorithm were obtained during spontaneous activity in the animals’ home cages and did not depend on specific experimental manipulations ([^15], [^48]).

Comparison of several paradigm-specific network and spatial coding features that were not used for training the decision tree, such as place field properties and spike-LFP phase correlations, also yielded high correlations between opto-tagged and inferred neuron types, adding further confidence to the reliability of physiology-based neuronal fingerprinting ([^21]) and enabling the study of interneuronal interactions during spatial learning. Although the correspondence between the synaptic coupling obtained by optogenetic manipulations and the functional coupling estimated by MI or GLMs ([Fig. 3D](#F3) and [fig. S6D](#SD2)) validates our approach to describe neuronal interactions, further studies will need to incorporate additional factors, including possible third-party interactions and oscillation phase–specific effects ([^48], [^49]).

Although the general importance of inhibition in circuit operations, and more specifically in spatial representation, has been discussed in numerous experimental and theoretical studies ([^23], [^24], [^32], [^50] - [^52]), our results support the “division of labor” across the various interneuron families ([^11], [^12]). The activity of *Pvalb* but not that of *Id2* neurons, both exhibiting high firing rate stability, showed a strong correlation with place cell stability. The degree of generalization between left and right trials of place fields of pyramidal cells covaried with generalizing properties of *Sst* neurons and, to a lesser extent, with those of *Pvalb* and *Vip* neurons ([^20], [^51], [^53]). The maze-arm generalization property of *Pvalb* and *Sst* interneurons can be explained by their dense innervation by pyramidal cells ([^54]) because arm-specific pyramidal neurons could equally discharge them ([^55]). Conversely, the relatively high context specificity of *Id2* interneurons can be explained by their sparse innervation by pyramidal cells ([^56]) because arm-specific (“splitter”) properties of unidentified interneurons varied inversely with the number of pyramidal splitter cells innervating them ([^57]). Space-rate MI ([^9], [^43], [^58]) was highest in pyramidal cells and *Vip* interneurons ([^59]). Selectivity measures were highest and lowest for *Vip* and *Sst* interneurons, respectively. Our family-specific perturbation experiments validated these correlational results. *Id2* interneurons profoundly inhibited both *Sst* and *Pvalb* and, less so, pyramidal cells and *Vip* interneurons. The strongest inhibitory effect of *Vip* was exerted on *Sst*, in line with anatomical observations that showed the strongest innervation of *Sst* interneurons, particularly OLM cells, by *Vip* interneurons ([^58], [^60] - [^61]). Further studies are needed to identify the physiological fingerprints of the various subgroups within each of these main interneuron families ([^11], [^17], [^21], [^24], [^34]).

Simultaneous recording from members of four families of interneurons allowed us to examine their cooperative interactions. The complex dynamics of their interactions were demonstrated by the observation that manipulating members of one family had differential effects on members of the other interneuron families and pyramidal neurons. Optogenetic activation of *Vip* neurons enhanced the place field firing of pyramidal cells most effectively, likely through suppressing *Sst* neurons ([^28], [^62]). Optogenetic activation of *Pvalb* neurons predominantly suppressed the first half of the place fields, whereas *Sst* and *Id2* activation suppressed the second half. These combined gain- and loss-of-function manipulations support the hypothesis that when the animal enters the place field, the entorhinal input is the main source of excitation, but it is gradually weakened by the potentiation of the place cell–OLM (*Sst*) interneuron synapse ([^15]). Conversely, depression of the place cell– *Pvalb* transmission probability allows the CA3 input to become the dominant source of place cell excitation in the second part of the place field ([^63]). Overall, our findings reveal a fundamental role for the cooperative function of interneuron families in the CA1 network in the emergence and rearrangement of the hippocampal cognitive map. Because our physiological fingerprinting strategy is paradigm independent, it is generalizable to datasets collected in different experiments and laboratories as well as to nonspatial functions of the hippocampus and, potentially, to human electrophysiological recordings ([^64], [^65]).

## Materials and methods

### Subject details and electrode implantation

All experiments were authorized by the Institutional Animal Care and Use Committee (IACUC) at New York University Medical Center or the Barcelona Biomedical Research Park (PRBB) Animal Research Ethics Committee and the local government and follow EU directive 2010/63/EU and Spanish guidelines (Laws 32/2007, 6/2013, and Real Decreto 53/2013). Transgenic mice expressing excitatory opsins (ChR2) under distinct promoters were created to target all five neuronal classes (Pvalb-Cre::Ai32, Sst-Flip::Ai80F, Id2-CreER/Dlx5-Flpe::Ai80, Vip-Flip:: Ai80F, CaMK2α-Cre::Ai32). Thirty-two male mice (with a primarily C57BL/6 genetic background, from 12 to 32 weeks of age) were used in this study. Mice were housed in the vivarium on a standard 12-hour light-dark cycle and were kept four to five per cage before surgery and individually after it. Mice (8 Pvalb-Cre::Ai32 mice, 3 Sst-Flip::Ai80F mice, 7 Id2-CreER/Dlx5-Flpe::Ai80 mice, 10 Vip-Flip::Ai80F mice, and 6 CaMK2α-Cre::Ai32 mice) were anesthetized with 2% isoflurane and maintained under anesthesia with 0.75 to 1% isoflurane. Animals were implanted with either 64-site, five-shank silicon probes (A5x12/16\_buz/lin\_5mm-100-200-160/177-H64LP\_30mm, with one middle 3.4-mm-long shank with 16 linear electrodes spaced 100 μm, and four 3-mm shanks with 12 staggered electrodes separated by 20 to 37 μm, NeuroNexus Technologies) together with an optogenetic fiber (105-μm diameter, Thorlabs) glued to the probe over the middle shank (~100 μm above the 13th channel) or with 32-site, four-shank μLED probes \[([^66]) eight staggered electrodes per shank spaced 20 to 40 μm, Neurolight, MI\], as indicated, over the dorsal right hippocampus (AP 2.0 mm, ML 1.5, DV 0.7 mm), as previously described ([^9], [^10], [^46], [^67]). Silicon probes were mounted on resin three-dimensional (3D)–printed microdrives, allowing precise vertical movement after implantation ([^68]), which were advanced to CA1 pyramidal layer in small increments over 5 to 8 days, while depth distribution of LFPs (SPW-R events and theta oscillations) and unit firing were used to identify CA1 pyramidal layer. Animals were allowed to recover for at least 2 weeks. Ground and reference wires were implanted in the skull above the cerebellum, and a grounded copper mesh was constructed to shield the probe.

### Recordings and behavior

Electrophysiological data were acquired using an Intan RHD2000 (Intan Technologies LLC) or Open Ephys adquisition board and digitized at 30 kHz. The wide-band signal was down-sampled to 1.25 kHz and used as the LFP signal. Animal’s position was monitored with a Basler camera (acA1300-60 gmNIR, Graftek Imaging) sampling at 30 Hz to detect a head-mounted red LED. Position was synchronized with neural data with transistor-transistor logic (TTLs) signaling shutter position. Animals were handled daily and accommodated to the experimenter, recording room, and cables for 1 week before the start of the experiments. After surgery, mice were moved to a 12-hour reverse light cycle (light on/off at 7pm/am) and housed individually. Before behavior training, mice were provided food and water ad libitum, but were water restricted to maintain 85% of their weight during and after behavioral training. Water access was restricted and was only available as reward on a spatial alteration task, ad libitum for 30 min at the end of each experimental day and ad libitum for 1 full nonexperimental day per week. Mice were trained to run laps in a polyvinyl chloride (PVC) spatial alteration maze to retrieve water reward (5 to 10 μl) at each arm of the maze. The maze was custom-built in the laboratory with dimension 70 cm × 40 cm (1 m above the floor) and 3.7- to 4.5-mm-high walls (McMaster-Carr, PVC U-Channel no. 80565K55; 2.5-mm-thick white polypropylene; see [Fig. 3](#F3)). Water was delivered by solenoids (Cole-Parmer, EW-98302) and activated by the animal crossing infrared beams (NOYITO E180D80NK) at the reward zones. Water delivery, maze doors, and optogenetic stimulation during track were controlled by a custom-made, Arduino-based circuit \[([^9], [^10]) circuits and software are available in [https://github.com/valegarman/HippoPlayground](https://github.com/valegarman/HippoPlayground)\]. For a typical recording session, mice were recorded continuously for about 170 min through three experimental blocks: prebaseline (60 to 80 min), spatial alternation task (30 min), and postbaseline (60 to 80 min).

For optogenetic tagging of specific neuron families, blue laser light (450 nm, Osram) or blue μLEDs pulses were delivered in the cortex or the hippocampus. For blue laser light, the maximum light power at the tip of the optic fiber was 1 to 4 mW. Then, 1- to 20-ms light pulses with 70% of the maximum power were delivered (*n* = 500 to 1000 times at each intensity, at 400 ± 200–ms random intervals). μLEDs were controlled with current \[2 to 4.5 μA generating 0.02 to 0.1 μW of total light power ([^69])\], provided by a 12-channel current generator (OSC1Lite, NeuroNex Michigan Hub) driven by an Arduino ([https://github.com/valegarman/HippoPlayground](https://github.com/valegarman/HippoPlayground)), which delivered trapezoid (1-ms rise time) blue light (centered emission at 460 nm, emission surface area = 150 mm <sup>2</sup>) 20-ms pulses at random sites with a randomly variable (40 to 60 ms) offset. No two or more light sites were stimulated at the same time.

### Unit clustering

Spike sorting was performed semiautomatically with KiloSort \[([^70]) [https://github.com/cortex-lab/KiloSort](https://github.com/cortex-lab/KiloSort)\], using the KilosortWrapper pipeline ([https://github.com/brendonw1/KilosortWrapper](https://github.com/brendonw1/KilosortWrapper)), as previously described ([^9], [^10], [^46]). This was followed by manual adjustment of the waveform clusters using the software Phy ([https://github.com/kwikteam/phy](https://github.com/kwikteam/phy)) and designed plugins for Phy ([https://github.com/petersenpeter/phy2-plugins](https://github.com/petersenpeter/phy2-plugins)). The following parameters were used for KiloSort: pos.Nfilt: 6 \*numberChannels; ops.nt0: 64; ops.whitening: ‘fulll’; ops.nSkipCov: 1; ops.whiteningRange: 64; ops.criterionNoiseChannels: 0.00001; ops.Nrank: 3; ops.nfullpasses: 6; ops.maxFR: 20000; ops.fshigh: 300; ops.ntbuff: 64; ops.scaleproc: 200; ops.Th: \[4 10 10\]; ops.lam: \[5 20 20\]; ops.nannealpasses: 4; ops.momentum: 1./\[20 800\]; ops.shuffle\_clusters: 1.

Units were assigned to specific layers in both the hippocampus and cortex based on the minimum distance between the recording channel and the nearest anatomical layer, and subsequently all assignments were manually curated. In the cortex, the layer identity was calculated as previously described ([^25], [^67]). In brief, we aligned depth profiles of electrophysiological landmarks. These included the largest amplitude peak of high-frequency LFP power (500 Hz to 5 kHz) corresponding to mid-layer 5 and four prominent sinks and sources from the averaged down-up current-source density (CSD) maps for each animal. In the hippocampus, layer identity was calculated as previously described ([^46]). *Stratum oriens* was defined as the channel with the highest theta power above the *stratum pyramidale*. *Stratum pyramidale* was defined as the recording channel with the largest-amplitude SPW-R. *Stratum radiatum* was defined by the prominent sink of the CSD during SPW-R. Finally, *stratum lacunosum-moleculare* was defined as the channel with the highest theta power.

To quantify the irregularity of neuronal firing, we computed the CV2 metric from the interspike intervals (ISIs) of each spike train. CV2 provides a local estimate of spike time variability and is defined for each pair of consecutive ISIs as

$$
\text{CV} 2_{i} = \frac{2 \mid \text{ISI}_{i + 1} - \text{ISI}_{i} \mid}{\text{ISI}_{i + 1} + \text{ISI}_{i}}
$$

The overall CV2 value for a neuron is the mean of all $\text{CV} 2_{i}$ values.

### Brain state scoring

Brain state scoring was performed as in ([^71]) with the MATLAB resource SleepScoreMaster from the repository of the laboratory [https://github.com/valegarman/HippoCookBook/tree/main/external\_pack-ages/SleepScoreMaster](https://github.com/valegarman/HippoCookBook/tree/main/external_pack-ages/SleepScoreMaster). In short, spectrograms were constructed with a 1-s sliding 10-s window FFT of 1250 Hz data at log-spaced frequencies between 1 and 100 Hz. Three types of signals were used for state scoring: broadband LFP, narrowband theta frequency LFP, and electromyogram (EMG). For broadband LFP signal, principal components analysis (PCA) was applied to the z-transformed (1 to 100 Hz) spectrogram. The first PC in all cases was based on power in the low (<20 Hz) frequency range and had negatively weighted power in the (>32 Hz) higher frequencies. Theta dominance was taken to be the ratio of 5 to 10 Hz and 2 to 16 Hz power from the spectrogram. EMG was extracted from the intracranially recorded signals (using a Butterworth filter with a bandwidth of 300 to 600 Hz and filter shoulders spanning 275 to 625 Hz) recorded at all sites ([^72]). The state scoring algorithm was performed by a series of divisions with thresholds set at the trough between the peaks of distributions in these three matrices. After automated brain scoring, all states were manually reviewed by the experimenter and minor corrections were made when discrepancies between automated scoring and user assessment occurred.

### Optogenetic light stimulation analysis

To evaluate optogenetic responses, we computed the cross-correlogram between the spikes of each neuron and the times of optogenetic stimulation. A bin size of 1 ms was used, and a window of 1 s was applied, centered around each stimulation event. Neurons were classified as optically tagged (opto-tagged) based on combination of four criteria ([^9]): (i) an average firing response higher than 2SD, (ii) significant modulation using a *P* value cutoff of 10 <sup>−3</sup>, (iii) against randomly shuffled pulse times (500 replicates) and testing for significant difference between the observed value and the random distribution, and (iv) a stimulus associated spike latency test (SALT) with statistically test the variance of jitter of the first spike after stimulation onset against a null distribution ([^73]).

### SPW-R detection

SPW-Rs were detected as previously described ([^9], [^46]), and the code (rippleMasterDetector) is available at the laboratory GitHub repository ([https://github.com/valegarman/HippoCookBook](https://github.com/valegarman/HippoCookBook)). Briefly, the raw LFP (1250 Hz) was filtered (130 to 200 Hz; Butterworth; order = 3) and was transformed to a normalized squared signal (NSS). The signal was used to identify peaks that were more than 5 standard deviations (SD) above the mean NSS. The beginning and end of the ripple were defined by a threshold of 2 SD above the mean SSD. SPW-R duration limits were between 20 and 200 ms. In addition, estimated EMG from the LFP ([^71]) was used to exclude EMG-related artifacts. The peak of the ripple (max power value >5 SD) was defined as time 0 for the ripple.

### Topological analysis of ripples

Topological analysis of ripples was computed as described in ([^74]). Briefly, the projection of the ripples waveforms in a high-dimensional state determined by the temporal sampling rate was constructed. To build the high-dimensional space, LFP signals (1250 Hz) from the channels where ripples were detected were cut into ±25-ms windows around the peak of the detected and filtered SWR (rounded to 63 points). Projecting all ripples into the 63D space (one dimension per sample, one point per SWR) resulted in a data cloud, which could be recovered into a low-dimensional space. We computed the number of action potentials in each time point for each neuron, and the mean *z* -scored transformation of all sessions for each neuron was applied. By using a Uniform Manifold Approximation and Projection for Dimension Reduction (UMAP) ([^75]) ([https://mathworks.com/matlab-central/fileexchange/71902](https://mathworks.com/matlab-central/fileexchange/71902)), the data from the 63D high-dimensional space was projected into a low-dimensional 4D space, as in ([^74]). Parameters for running the UMAP were min\_dist: 0.1; metric: euclidean; n\_neighbors: 15; n\_components = 4. The structure index (SI) was used to quantify the amount of structure the projection of each neuron feature presents over the data cloud \[([^74]) [https://github.com/PridaLab/structure\_index](https://github.com/PridaLab/structure_index)\].

### Phase locking

Phase locking was calculated as previously described ([^9], [^46]). For theta phase locking, LFP from CA1 *stratum oriens* (highest theta power channel above the CA1 pyramidal layer) was band-pass filtered (5 to 12 Hz) and the instantaneous phase was determined using a Hilbert transform. Only epochs with theta power above 1 SD were selected for analysis. Theta phase locking was determined by calculating the phase angles for each time stamp where an action potential occurred [https://github.com/valegarman/HippoCookBook/blob/main/analysis/LFP\_spikes\_modulation/computePhaseModulation.m](https://github.com/valegarman/HippoCookBook/blob/main/analysis/LFP_spikes_modulation/computePhaseModulation.m). A histogram of phase angles was calculated, and the circular mean and resultant vector were calculated for each neuron. The same approach was used for computing slow, mid and fast gamma phase modulation. Only epochs of gamma power exceeding 1 SD were selected for analysis. For slow-gamma, LFP from CA1 *stratum radiatum* (defined by the maximum CSD sink during SPW-Rs) was band-filtered (20 to 50 Hz) and the instantaneous phase was determined using a Hilbert transform. For mid-gamma, LFP from CA1 *stratum oriens* (highest theta power channel above the CA1 pyramidal layer) was band-pass filtered (60 to 100 Hz). Finally, for fast gamma, LFP from CA1 *pyramidal layer* was band-pass filtered (100 to 140 Hz).

### Place field analysis

Rate maps were generated by binning spiking data into 2.2-cm-wide bins, and spike counts were normalized by time occupancy (smoothing size: 2 bins) for periods when the animal’s speed was >1 cm/s. Maps for left- and right-bound trials, and stimulation and nonstimulation trials were generated separately. Place fields were defined from these maps by finding peaks in the rate maps. The field boundaries were defined until the rate was above 20% of the peak firing rate. Place fields cleared all criteria if they were between 8.75-cm and 75-cm wide and had a minimum peak firing rate of 3 Hz and a spatial coherence >0.7 ([^38]). Place field stability was defined by correlating rate/bin distributions (Spearman’s correlation) for the first and second halves of trials in the maze. Additional fields were defined if they cleared the above description criterion. Place field generalization (context generalization) was computed as the correlation/ bin distribution (Spearman’s correlation) between the left and right trials. Place cell selectivity was estimated as follows

$$
\text{Place cell selectivity} = \frac{R_{\text{peak}}}{〈 R_{\text{avg}} 〉}
$$

where $R_{\text{peak}}$ is the maximum firing rate observed in the place cell’s rate map and $〈 R_{\text{avg}} 〉$ is the mean firing rate of the place cell across all spatial bins.

The spatial information encoded in place cells firing rates were calculated using three complementary approaches. MI between position and firing rate ([^42]) was estimated as follows

$$
\text{MI} = \sum_{i} \sum_{j} p_{i , j} log_{2} \left(\frac{p_{i , j}}{p_{i} \times p_{j}}\right)
$$

where $p_{i}$ and $p_{j}$ are the probabilities of the $i_{t h}$ position bin and the $j_{t h}$ firing rate bin, respectively, and $p_{i , j}$ is the joint probability between position $i_{t h}$ and firing rate $j_{t h}$. Spatial information in bits per centimeter was calculated as follows ([^76])

$$
\text{SPI} = \sum_{K} P_{K \mid x_{i}} log_{2} \frac{P_{K \mid x_{i}}}{P_{K}}
$$

where $P_{K}$ is the probability of observing a rate $K$ and $P_{K \mid x_{i}}$ is the conditional probability of observing a rate $K$ in position $x_{i}$. And spatial information in bits per spike was also calculated as follows

$$
\text{SPI} = \sum_{i} p_{i} \frac{\lambda_{i}}{\lambda} log_{2} \frac{\lambda_{i}}{\lambda}
$$

Population sparsity was computed using the sparsity index, estimated as follows

$$
\text{Sparsity} = \frac{\left[\sum \sum \left(\text{Occup} \times R\right)\right]^{2}}{\sum \sum \left(\text{Occup} \times R^{2}\right)}
$$

where $\text{Occup}$ represents the time the subject spends in each bin across different trials and $R$ is the neuron’s firing rate across trials.

For dimensionality reduction of neural trajectories and position decoding, we implemented the CEBRA toolbox \[([^44]) [https://cebra.ai](https://cebra.ai/)\]. First, the data were preprocessed as previously explained ([^44]). Specifically, the spikes were binned into 30-ms time windows and the position was encoded into a vector. Recordings were split into train, validation, and test sets with a *k* = 3 nested cross-validation schemes for the decoding task ([^44]).

### Decision tree classifier

For classifying neuron families, we fitted a binary decision tree for multiclass classification with parameters optimization during 10-fold cross-validation by using the MATLAB function *fitctree* with the option “OptimizeHyperparameters” in mode “on” for hyperparameter optimization during the 10-fold cross-validation. For cross-validation, the dataset was randomly divided into 10 equal-sized subsets, and the model was trained on 9 of the 10 subsets and tested on the remaining 1 subset. This process was repeated 10 times, with each of the 10 subsets serving as the test set once. The performance of the model was evaluated by averaging the results from each fold, and the parameters. After each model fit, feature importance for each feature was computed by averaging the predictive measure of association between predictors using the MATLAB built-in function “predictorImportance,” considering both optimal and surrogate splits in a decision tree, and normalizing by the total number of splits for the optimal predictor. All pretrained models are available in our open toolbox, [https://github.com/valegarman/HippoCookBook/tree/main/analysis/](https://github.com/valegarman/HippoCookBook/tree/main/analysis/), in the subfolder “cell\_type\_classification.”

### Functional coupling

Functional coupling between units was estimated by their MI content or by fitting a GLM. Briefly, MI was estimated using the Neuroscience Information Theory Toolbox ([https://github.com/nmtimme/Neuroscience-Information-Theory-Toolbox](https://github.com/nmtimme/Neuroscience-Information-Theory-Toolbox)). Spiking data were counted into 10-ms bins and discretized into states using either four uniform count bins (regions) or two uniform count bins (single units). The MI was then computed for each time bin as follows

The significance of MI estimation was tested by randomly shuffling the spike trains (500 times). Graphs were constructed from adjacency matrices at a threshold of 0.01 bits. For GLM, spiking data were counted in 10-ms bins, and the spike train of each neuron was modeled as a function of the other simultaneously recorded neurons’ activity iteratively, including lagged effects and interactions. Once the GLM was fitted, the model coefficients, represent the strength of the functional coupling between neurons. Only pairs of neurons with a significant (*P* ≤ 0.05) interaction, estimated by a likelihood ratio test.
