# The Brain's Default Network: Anatomy, Function, and Relevance to Disease

Buckner RL, Andrews-Hanna JR, Schacter DL. (2008). *Annals of the New York Academy of Sciences*, 1124:1–38.

Canonical anatomy review establishing the DMN as a defined brain system, mapping its core regions, two-subsystem architecture, and roles in spontaneous cognition and disease.

---

## Key Computational Insights

- **Two-subsystem architecture**: DMN splits into (1) a *medial temporal subsystem* (HC, RSC, PHC, lateral temporal cortex — scene/memory construction) and (2) a *medial prefrontal subsystem* (dMPFC, TPJ, temporal pole — self-referential and social cognition); both converge at PCC and vmPFC hubs.
- **Sentinel hypothesis**: DMN performs ongoing environmental monitoring for personally relevant events even during rest — an always-on salience filter that redirects attention when a relevant signal is detected; distinct from the Salience Network trigger.
- **Competitive interactions**: DMN and task-positive networks (dorsal attention / FPN) are anti-correlated; failure of this suppression is a proposed mechanism in Alzheimer's and autism.
- **Metabolic baseline**: DMN nodes (PCC, mPFC) display disproportionately high resting glucose metabolism and uniform OEF (Oxygen Extraction Fraction) at rest — evidence of costly, always-on processing rather than passive inactivity.
- **HC as core DMN node**: HC couples to mPFC via RSC; this loop drives scene construction, episodic retrieval, and episodic future simulation — the memory substrate for frames of thought.
- **Alzheimer's amyloid-metabolism link**: Amyloid plaques preferentially deposit in DMN nodes; the "metabolism hypothesis" proposes chronically elevated DMN metabolic activity creates conditions for amyloid accumulation; HC-DMN decoupling is an early AD biomarker.

---

## Limitations

- Based on fMRI/PET — temporal resolution insufficient to resolve sub-second frame dynamics.
- Disease associations are correlational; causal direction of the amyloid-metabolism link is debated.
- Subsystem boundaries are connectivity-derived, not anatomically discrete — boundaries shift with task context.
