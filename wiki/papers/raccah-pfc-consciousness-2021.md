# Does the Prefrontal Cortex Play an Essential Role in Consciousness?

Raccah O, Block N, Fox KCR — *J Neuroscience* 41(10):2076–2087, 2021. PMC8018764.

Synthesizes intracranial electrical stimulation (iES) evidence across decades of research to causally test whether PFC activity *constitutes* conscious experience.

---

- **iES elicitation gradient confirms null lateral PFC.** Across 67 patients and 1537 electrode sites, anterior/lateral PFC (dlPFC, frontopolar BA-10) shows the lowest elicitation rate in the entire brain — near zero; unimodal sensory cortex reaches ~67%. Frontopolar cortex has never yielded a confirmed phenomenal report in the literature. iES is causal; neuroimaging is correlational and confounded by post-perceptual cognitive activity.
- **OFC and ACC are the only reliable PFC loci.** OFC stimulation produces olfactory, gustatory, somatosensory, and emotional experiences (17% elicitation rate; 83% null; anterior OFC completely silent; posterior-to-anterior gradient). ACC stimulation produces emotional regulation, motivational feelings, visceral and fear experiences; experience intensity scales linearly with stimulation amplitude. Neither profile matches a general perceptual broadcasting role.
- **Constitutive vs. enabling distinction is decisive.** PFC may be a global *enabling* condition for consciousness (arousal, attention, WM) without being *constitutive* of it. iES tests constitutiveness directly: disrupting an enabling factor leaves percepts intact; disrupting a constitutive factor changes them. Lateral PFC disruption leaves percepts intact — consistent with enabling, not constitutive.
- **Dense distributed PFC codes explain the null result mechanistically.** PFC neurons carry mixed-selectivity, distributed codes spanning large circuits; disrupting a small iES patch leaves the population-level representation intact. Unimodal sensory regions use sparse topographic codes — patch disruption alters the percept directly. This is a behavioral correlate of the CCGP/SD representational geometry, not a methodological artifact.
- **Challenge to GNW and higher-order theories (HOT).** Both predict lateral PFC stimulation should alter or impede ongoing conscious perception (disrupting the hub, or disrupting the second-order re-representation). No such effects are observed across hundreds of studies. IIT's prediction — fewer PFC effects due to local low-Φ connectivity — is the theory most consistent with this data.

---

**Limitations:** All patients have neurological disease (epilepsy, tumors, psychiatric illness); compensatory plasticity unknown but seizure-free tissue shows normal stimulus responses. Subtle behavioral effects on unreported tasks cannot be ruled out. Dense PFC circuits may require non-local perturbation to produce effects — iES may be inherently underpowered for distributed representations. Stimulation parameters vary across studies.
