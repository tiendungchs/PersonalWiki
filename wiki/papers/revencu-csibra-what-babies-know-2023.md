# Review of Spelke's *What Babies Know* — Revencu & Csibra, Mind & Language 2023

**Citation:** Revencu, B. & Csibra, G. (2023). The missing link between core knowledge and language: Review of Elizabeth Spelke's *What Babies Know*, volume 1 (2022). *Mind & Language* 38, 1314–1322. doi:10.1111/mila.12482

A critical commentary. Endorses core knowledge; disputes Spelke's claim that **natural-language acquisition alone** explains the productivity of human thought.

---

## Key Insights

- **Six core domains (Spelke 2022 vol. 1), not four.** Trackable objects (cohesion/continuity/action-on-contact), navigable spatial layouts, magnitudes of sets/sequences, shapes and object kinds, instrumental agents (efficient action), and social beings (mutual engagement). Extends the 2007 taxonomy.
- **Core systems combine two features that jointly block cross-domain thought:** (1) **modularity** — operation is insulated from the organism's beliefs; (2) **attention-dependence** — activation is not automatic and competes for limited attentional resources. Together these prevent representations like "Mary broke the five bottles she liked," which requires pooling ≥3 core systems simultaneously.
- **Spelke's productivity engine = natural language.** Syntax composes units; compositional semantics interprets the whole from parts + combination rules. Linguistic expressions can *point to* core representations without consuming attention. She **rejects an innate language of thought (LoT)** because it would cause combinatorial explosion (the infant would not know which concepts/propositions are useful or true).
- **The reviewers' central objection — bidirectional links between language and core systems do not obtain:**
  - Core concepts are *taken for granted* and rarely spoken → adult utterances cannot supply them.
  - Many function words (logical operators, tense/aspect, modals) have *no* core-system counterpart → language cannot bootstrap them from core primitives.
  - ⇒ an innate LoT is needed as the interface after all.
- **Combinatorial-explosion double bind.** Spelke rejects LoT to avoid explosion, but a productive natural-language composer faces the *same* problem: if composition is internally driven, why is SOCIAL AGENT composed *first* rather than any of myriad possible combinations? The account "only pushes the problem slightly further in development."
- **Representational-format equivocation.** If language is needed to bring core outputs into a *common* format, then core concepts are a *different kind* than the concepts used as building blocks of thought — so they cannot be straightforwardly used as compositional primitives. A codec between the two formats is required and not provided.
- **Core systems are not strictly unitary/all-or-none.** Stahl & Feigenson (2015): infants selectively explore an object by *which* physical principle it violated (bang a solidity-violator, drop a gravity-violator) rather than suspending all objecthood. Spatiotemporal continuity dissociates from cohesion (teleportation) and action-on-contact is violable (remote controls) without demoting objecthood. Undercuts Spelke's use of "unitary/all-or-none" as a criterion.
- **Association vs. productive composition — an under-specified boundary.** Spelke grants animals a limited *associative* combination and reserves *productive* composition for language-endowed humans, but offers no criterion to tell which a given infant behavior reflects (e.g. is SOCIAL AGENT productive, or just "efficient agents tend to engage"?).

---

## Limitations

- A commentary on volume 1 of a two-volume work; the authors defer to a promised elaborated account in volume 2.
- Argues *against* the language-only account but offers no worked-out LoT-plus-codec model of its own — the positive proposal is a gap it identifies, not one it fills.
- Developmental-psychology evidence (looking-time, individuation studies); no computational model.
