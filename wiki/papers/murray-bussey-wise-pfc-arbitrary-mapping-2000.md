# Role of prefrontal cortex in a network for arbitrary visuomotor mapping

Murray, E.A., Bussey, T.J. & Wise, S.P. (2000). *Experimental Brain Research* 133:114–129. doi:10.1007/s002210000406. The long companion review to [[wiki/papers/wise-murray-arbitrary-mapping-2000.md]] (*TINS*, same year, overlapping authors): same evidence base, but this is where the **architectural theory** lives.

Full treatment in [[wiki/concepts/arbitrary-mapping.md]].

## Key computational insights

- **Cortical-BG modules as the computational unit** (Houk & Wise 1995): recurrent cortex→BG→thalamus→cortex loops running largely in **parallel**; each cortical area participates in many. **PM modules** compute specific object→action mappings; **PF modules** compute *the same* mappings **plus** higher-order rules and strategies. The division is not "PF abstracts / PM executes" — PF has higher-order functions *in addition to* lower-order ones.
- **Higher- vs. lower-order rules.** Lower-order = an exemplar (red square → leftward joystick). Higher-order = the abstraction over exemplars ("nonspatial visual information instructs the action"). **Critically, the authors reject the necessity claim themselves:** each exemplar "could be learned, one by one, without ever encoding the general rule," so higher-order rules cannot explain the PFv+o deficit. They buy **speed and efficiency**, not capability.
- **Strategy is a third, dissociable thing.** The **Repeat-Stay/Change-Shift** strategy solves repeat trials outright and halves change trials, cutting initial error from 67%→33% on a 3-choice task. **PFv+o lesions abolish it; hippocampal lesions spare it** — HS-lesioned monkeys keep applying RS/CS despite failing to learn new mappings. Even so, strategy loss alone does not explain the PFv+o deficit: it predicts ~67% initial errors, not the observed failure to learn *at all* within 50-trial blocks.
- **The disconnection hypothesis.** PFv+o may support mapping not (only) as a store but as a **route**: IT (incl. perirhinal) supplies most of the frontal lobe's object-vision input and **does not project directly to PM**, so a PFv+o lesion can sever IT→PM. Evidence it is not purely a store: PF is insufficient without PMd. The two mechanisms are explicitly **not mutually exclusive**.
- **PF is not a working-memory module.** Rushworth et al. 1997: PFv is unnecessary for 8s delayed matching. In the key experiment the instruction stimulus **stayed on-screen through choice and execution** — near-zero storage demand — yet the deficit was profound. The authors call this a falsification of "the widely promoted hypothesis that the sole specialized function of PF, as a whole, is the mediation of working memory."
- **Lesion matrix** (Table 1). PFv+o: higher-order rules/strategies **lost**, novel mappings **lost**, familiar partially preserved (relearned across sessions). PFdl: familiar preserved, novel partially disrupted. PM: both lost. BG output: both lost. HS: rules/strategies **and** familiar preserved, novel severely disrupted.
- **HS = intermediate-term store**, holding mappings, rules, *and* strategies in parallel pending consolidation into frontal cortical-BG modules — the long-term store.
- **Perseveration and WM are ruled out as accounts** of the PFv+o deficit: post-lesion repetition of an incorrect response was no more likely than any alternative response, and no tendency to repeat a rewarded response on change trials was found.
- **Generality beyond visuomotor:** uncinate-fascicle transection impairs arbitrary visuo*visual* mapping; reward→stimulus mapping engages IT/PF too (Parker & Gaffan 1998). But IT/PF disconnection spares visual discrimination and configural learning — the authors argue those are solved by *standard* mapping (approach a high-valence target), not arbitrary mapping.

## Limitations

Rodent homology is the stated weak point ("the difficulty in identifying homologous frontal areas in rats and monkeys"), leaving the rat AGm/AGi results hard to align with the monkey PF/PM split. **Every rat study reviewed used two-choice tasks, which cannot dissociate strategy use from mapping acquisition** — the authors flag this as a possible explanation for discrepant rat HC results and call for three-choice designs. Cerebellar involvement is reported clinically but absent in monkey lesions, and set aside. Some key results are unpublished observations by the authors at the time of writing. The cortical-BG module architecture is imported as a framework (Houk & Wise 1995), not tested here; no computational model or simulation is offered, and the proposed PF/PM specializations remain interpretations of a lesion matrix.
