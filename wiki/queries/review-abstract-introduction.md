# Review Article — Abstract & Introduction

> Drafted from the current state of this wiki (185 papers, 57 concepts, 33 model entities) as the opening of a review article. Synthesis, not a filed answer to a user question; `(brainstorm)` framing applies to the architectural claims that go beyond established results.

## Abstract

Deep learning has automated perception and interpolation but still fails, systematically and predictably, at the thing biological cognition does effortlessly: out-of-distribution *systematic generalization* — applying a learned rule to genuinely novel content. This review advances a single organizing hypothesis for why brains succeed where fixed-depth feedforward networks cannot, and for how to close the gap. We frame every reasoning task — analogy, planning, mathematical proof, spatial navigation, program induction — as one problem, **latent graph discovery**: infer the hidden relational structure (nodes, edges, topology) of a domain from observations, then navigate it, where the graph is never given explicitly. Task types differ only in which graph component is latent (edges, path, node content, or topology), yielding a taxonomy that places ARC-AGI, FrontierMath, GPQA, and adversarial NLI on one axis and exposes a sixth, qualitatively harder regime — *non-stationary topology*, where the edge set rewrites mid-episode (rule-changing games such as Baba Is AI, Fluxx, Nomic). We argue the enabling architectural principle is **factorization of structural knowledge from sensory content** (grid-like structural code vs. content code), implemented as a **two-timescale learning system** — a slow, transferable meta-graph (cortical/entorhinal weights *W*) and a fast, episode-specific instance-graph (hippocampal Hebbian memory *M*) — and governed by a basal-ganglia/prefrontal control layer that gates, modulates, and hierarchically structures inference, with a default-mode network supplying the slowly changing contextual frame. We show this frame unifies a striking range of otherwise disconnected results: the Tolman-Eichenbaum Machine equals a transformer with factorized memory; grid cells equal successor-representation eigenvectors and optimal path-integration bases; contrastive Hebbian learning at energy equilibrium computes exact gradients without a backward pass; planning reduces to attractor inference over a spacetime-embedded world model rather than sequential rollout; and a formal *intelligence-density* criterion separates architectures that can "know" a scaling domain (recurrent/iterative) from those provably bounded to memorization (feedforward). We close by cataloguing where the program is still open — transformation inference from before/after pairs, vocabulary co-discovery, biologically plausible slow-*W* at scale, the objectness prior, workspace update, and online topology re-inference — and argue that convergent evolution of the same expansion→compression circuit across at least five independent lineages is the strongest available evidence that this motif is near-optimal for the underlying computation.

## Introduction

### The generalization gap is architectural, not a matter of scale

The dominant empirical fact motivating this review is a dissociation. Contemporary large models achieve superhuman interpolation yet collapse on benchmarks built to require novel *rule application*: ARC-AGI-2 resists brute-force search (frontier systems <3%), and adversarial NLI drops hypothesis-only baselines from 72% to 42–51% the moment the spurious shortcut edge is blocked — with *larger* models often *more* susceptible, an inverse-scaling signature that scale alone cannot explain. The wiki's position, developed across [[wiki/concepts/neural-manifolds.md]] and [[wiki/concepts/intelligence-density.md]], is that this is a boundary of representational reachability, not of training budget: relational generalization lies *outside* a standard transformer's intrinsic manifold, so no amount of data moves the model onto it. Formally, a system "knows" a domain when its intelligence density $\mathcal{I} = \log_2 N / C$ diverges as the domain scales — a criterion recurrent and iterative architectures can meet but fixed-depth feedforward networks are provably bounded away from. The problem is what the architecture *can represent*, not what it has *seen*.

### One problem underneath many tasks: latent graph discovery

We resist treating analogy, planning, arithmetic, and navigation as separate capabilities. Each is an instance of inferring and then traversing a relational graph that is never handed to the solver ([[wiki/concepts/latent-graph-discovery.md]]). What distinguishes tasks is *which component is hidden*: transformation rules (edges) in ARC-AGI and IQ analogies; the path in navigation and multi-step proof; node content in constraint satisfaction and graduate-level causal-chain reasoning; the full topology in scientific and causal discovery. This reframing does real work — it predicts that the residual difficulty of ARC, once binding and path integration are solved, is *vocabulary co-discovery* (isolated cleanly by the VSA/HRR result: 94.5% with a pre-given DSL, 3% when the operator alphabet must itself be inferred) — and it exposes a sixth source of hardness the other five share an assumption against: **non-stationary topology**, where the edge set rewrites *within* a single episode. Rule-changing games (Baba Is AI, Fluxx, Nomic) are natural experiments in what keeps a mutating graph learnable; the answer is a recursion of the same two-timescale hierarchy, tractable only while the rewrite process is itself stationary.

### The architectural answer: factorize, then run two timescales under hierarchical control

The brain's solution, as reconstructed here, rests on a small set of principles ([[wiki/concepts/factorized-representations.md]], [[wiki/concepts/two-learning-timescales.md]]). Structure is factored from content — a content-free structural code (entorhinal grid cells) that generalizes across environments, bound to environment-specific sensory codes only transiently in the hippocampus. Learning runs on two clocks: a slow, backprop-like consolidation of the shared **meta-graph** into weights *W*, and a fast, one-shot Hebbian write of the **instance-graph** into memory *M*. Conflating these clocks forces relearning rules from scratch for every problem — the signature failure of monolithic networks. Above this sits a basal-ganglia/prefrontal control layer that performs opponent (Go/NoGo) credit assignment, gates storage versus retrieval, and searches a rostro-caudal hierarchy of rules-about-rules in parallel; a default-mode network ([[wiki/entities/default-mode-network.md]]) maintains the slowly changing "frame of thought" within which fast attention operates; and the whole stack runs on a repeated canonical cortical microcircuit ([[wiki/concepts/canonical-microcircuit.md]]) that amplifies weak inputs and selects hypotheses by winner-take-all.

### Why this synthesis is more than a metaphor

The frame earns its keep by unifying results that were not obviously related. The Tolman-Eichenbaum Machine ([[wiki/entities/tem-model.md]]) turns out to *be* a transformer whose factorized key/value structure follows necessarily from outer-product memory; grid cells are successor-representation eigenvectors and simultaneously the optimal basis for graph matching and multi-step planning; equilibrium propagation proves a purely local contrastive-Hebbian update computes exact gradients, dissolving the biological-plausibility objection to backprop in principle; planning becomes attractor *inference* over a world model embedded in recurrent weights ([[wiki/entities/spacetime-attractor.md]], [[wiki/concepts/planning-as-inference.md]]), avoiding rollout error; and memory schemas ([[wiki/concepts/memory-schemas.md]]) are the prefrontal biological instantiation of slow-*W* latent-graph storage. Convergent evolution supplies the capstone argument: at least five to six independent lineages derive the *same* expansion→compression circuit for allocentric world modeling, evidence that the motif is near-optimal rather than merely one workable choice.

### A measurement caveat and the open frontier

Any review in this area must confront a measurement problem: standard accuracy conflates genuine concept acquisition with **shortcut solutions** ([[wiki/concepts/shortcut-reasoning.md]]) — correct output, wrong reasoning — at rates around 27–29% on ARC (vs. ~5% for humans), inflating apparent abstraction and tracing to a missing objectness prior that token prediction never installs. We therefore treat benchmark scores as necessary but not sufficient evidence. The remainder of this review develops the frame in detail and then maps the genuinely open problems it leaves standing: transformation inference from before/after pairs (the critical missing module in all current factorized models), vocabulary co-discovery under cross-environment transfer, biologically plausible slow-*W* at scale, workspace release/update, and online re-inference of non-stationary topology. These are the load-bearing gaps between the current best understanding and a system that discovers latent graphs the way brains do.
